# Plant Functional Gene Research Reference

## 1. Scope

This file supports interpretation and review writing for literature on plant functional gene research, crop functional gene research, plant molecular biology, plant stress responses, gene families, plant population genetics, population genomics, evolutionary genomics, comparative genomics, plant multi-omics, and molecular breeding. Use it with source evidence, and separate source-reported content, reasonable inference, and model synthesis. Do not convert association evidence into causal conclusions.

## 2. Major Research Directions

- Growth and development regulation: plant height, branching or tillering, leaf morphology, vascular development, organ size, cell division, and cell expansion.
- Abiotic stress responses: drought, salinity, alkalinity, cold, heat, flooding, oxidative stress, heavy metals, and nutrient deficiency.
- Biotic stress resistance: pathogens, viruses, nematodes, insects, and plant immune responses.
- Hormone signaling pathways: ABA, IAA, GA, BR, CK, ETH, JA, SA, and SL signaling.
- Crop yield and quality traits: grain weight, panicle or spike architecture, seed size, fertility, oil, protein, starch, aroma, and nutritional quality.
- Photosynthesis and carbon/nitrogen metabolism: chloroplast development, photosynthetic efficiency, carbon assimilation, nitrogen uptake, nitrogen-use efficiency, and source-sink relationships.
- Root development and nutrient uptake: root architecture, lateral roots, adventitious roots, root hairs, mycorrhizal interactions, and uptake of phosphorus, nitrogen, potassium, iron, and other nutrients.
- Reproductive development and fertility: floral organs, pollen development, ovule development, self-incompatibility, male sterility, and hybrid breeding.
- Epigenetic and chromatin regulation: DNA methylation, histone modifications, chromatin accessibility, and three-dimensional genome regulation.
- Non-coding RNA regulation: miRNA, siRNA, lncRNA, circRNA, and target-gene regulatory networks.
- Gene families and evolution: gene duplication, conserved domains, phylogeny, expression divergence, subfunctionalization, and neofunctionalization.
- Domestication, natural variation, and molecular breeding: favorable alleles, haplotypes, selective sweeps, genetic improvement, and marker-assisted selection.
- Population genetics and population genomics: population structure, genetic diversity, linkage disequilibrium, demographic history, introgression, and selection signals.
- Evolutionary and comparative genomics: orthology, paralogy, synteny, whole-genome duplication, gene loss, positive selection, and cross-species conservation or divergence.
- Cell-type-specific function: single-cell profiles, spatial expression, cell lineages, tissue microenvironments, and cell-type-specific regulation.

## 3. Common Research Approaches

### Candidate Gene Discovery

- QTL: maps genomic regions associated with traits; usually requires fine mapping and functional validation.
- GWAS: identifies candidate loci in natural populations; interpret with attention to population structure, linkage disequilibrium, and multiple testing.
- BSA-seq: rapidly maps candidate regions using extreme phenotype bulks; useful for major-effect loci.
- MutMap: combines mutants and resequencing to identify candidate mutations; background mutations must be excluded.
- Gene family analysis: identifies family members, domains, phylogeny, and expression patterns; usually generates candidates rather than proving function.
- Comparative genomics: identifies conserved or lineage-specific genes, synteny, and evolutionary clues; it does not directly prove function.
- Haplotype analysis: links natural variation with trait differences; it can support breeding potential but requires causal validation.
- Population structure analysis: distinguishes ancestry, subgroups, and stratification; it does not identify causal genes by itself.
- Selection scan: uses metrics such as Fst, nucleotide diversity, Tajima's D, XP-CLR, or related statistics to detect candidate selected regions; selection signals require functional and historical interpretation.
- Pangenome analysis: identifies core, dispensable, presence/absence, and structural variation; variation catalogs require association or functional evidence for trait claims.

### Expression and Regulatory Analysis

- qRT-PCR: validates expression trends of candidate genes; it is not functional validation.
- RNA-seq: profiles genome-wide expression changes and differentially expressed genes.
- small RNA-seq: identifies miRNAs, siRNAs, and potential regulatory relationships.
- lncRNA/circRNA analysis: predicts non-coding RNAs, target genes, and regulatory networks.
- scRNA-seq: resolves cell-type-specific expression and cell-state differences.
- Spatial transcriptomics: preserves tissue spatial information and supports spatial expression interpretation.
- WGCNA: builds co-expression modules and links them to traits or treatments; it supports candidate prioritization.
- GO/KEGG: provides functional annotation and pathway enrichment clues; it is not mechanistic proof.
- promoter-GUS/LUC: tests promoter activity and tissue-specific or treatment-responsive expression.
- In situ hybridization: localizes transcripts in tissues or cells.

### Functional Validation

- Overexpression: tests phenotypes under elevated gene expression; non-physiological expression levels must be considered.
- RNAi: reduces gene expression to test phenotypes; off-target effects and silencing efficiency must be assessed.
- VIGS: provides rapid transient silencing; stable genetic materials are needed for stronger validation.
- CRISPR/Cas9: generates target-gene mutations; mutation type, genetic background, and off-target risk must be checked.
- Base editing: creates targeted base substitutions; editing efficiency and byproducts must be verified.
- Prime editing: enables more precise editing; editing efficiency and unintended products must be assessed.
- T-DNA: provides insertion mutants; insertion site and expression effects must be confirmed.
- EMS mutants: provide point-mutation resources; genetic analysis and candidate mutation validation are required.
- Complementation: restores a mutant phenotype using a wild-type gene; this is strong functional evidence.
- Allele replacement: tests the function of natural alleles or key causal sites.
- Transient expression: rapidly tests localization, regulation, interaction, or preliminary phenotypic effects; it does not fully replace stable genetic materials.

### Mechanistic Analysis

- Subcellular localization: identifies protein localization and supports cellular functional inference.
- Y2H: tests protein-protein interaction; additional validation is required.
- BiFC: visualizes protein interaction in cells; overexpression effects should be considered.
- Co-IP: tests in vivo protein complexes or interactions.
- Pull-down: tests in vitro protein interaction; recombinant systems have limitations.
- LUC: commonly tests promoter activity, transcriptional regulation, or interaction-related activity.
- EMSA: tests in vitro protein-DNA binding.
- ChIP-qPCR/ChIP-seq: tests in vivo chromatin binding sites.
- DAP-seq: identifies transcription factor binding sites in vitro at high throughput.
- ATAC-seq: profiles chromatin accessibility and potential regulatory regions.

### Multi-Omics

- Transcriptomics: profiles expression changes and regulatory candidates.
- Proteomics: measures protein abundance, which may differ from transcript abundance.
- Metabolomics: measures metabolite changes and supports pathway-level evidence.
- Epigenomics: profiles DNA methylation, chromatin state, and epigenetic regulation.
- Phosphoproteomics: identifies phosphorylation events and signaling clues.
- Multi-omics integration: combines expression, protein, metabolite, epigenetic, and phenotype evidence to build mechanistic models.

### Population, Evolutionary, and Comparative Genomics

- Population genomics: supports inference about diversity, structure, demographic history, introgression, and selection.
- Evolutionary genomics: supports inference about gene family evolution, duplication, loss, selection, and divergence.
- Comparative genomics: supports inference about synteny, conservation, orthology, paralogy, and lineage-specific innovation.
- These approaches generate evolutionary or association hypotheses; they do not prove molecular function without additional evidence.

### Phenotypic and Physiological Validation

- Morphological phenotypes: plant height, root length, leaf area, organ size, and developmental timing.
- Physiological indicators: chlorophyll, photosynthetic rate, stomatal conductance, ROS, MDA, proline, ion content, and related traits.
- Yield traits: grain number, grain weight, fertility, per-plant yield, and population yield.
- Quality traits: protein, oil, starch, sugar, fiber, and secondary metabolites.
- Stress treatments: report treatment concentration, duration, developmental stage, control, and recovery conditions.
- High-throughput phenotyping: supports large-scale trait measurement; algorithms, environment, and validation samples must be considered.

## 4. Evidence Hierarchy

Evidence strength generally increases through the following levels. Strength also depends on experimental design, replication, statistics, genetic background, and independent validation.

### sequence annotation only

Can support: functional prediction based on sequence, domain structure, homology, and phylogeny; useful for proposing candidate genes.

Cannot support: actual function in a target species, tissue, treatment, or trait.

### expression correlation

Can support: correlation between gene expression and tissue, developmental stage, treatment condition, or phenotype.

Cannot support: causal regulation of a trait or causal placement in a pathway.

### co-expression/network evidence

Can support: co-expression with modules, traits, or other genes; useful for candidate prioritization and network hypotheses.

Cannot support: direct regulation, protein interaction, or genetic causality.

### population or comparative genomics evidence

Can support: candidate regions, evolutionary conservation or divergence, selection signals, introgression hypotheses, or natural-variation associations.

Cannot support: gene function, adaptive mechanism, or causal trait effect without functional, statistical, or experimental validation.

### transient assay evidence

Can support: localization, transcriptional activation or repression, promoter response, protein interaction, or preliminary phenotypic effects in a transient system.

Cannot support: stable function under natural development or field environments by itself.

### genetic perturbation evidence

Can support: evidence that altered gene activity through overexpression, knockout, knockdown, editing, insertion mutation, or silencing affects phenotype.

Cannot support: exclusion of background effects or non-specific effects without complementation, independent alleles, multiple backgrounds, or off-target assessment.

### complementation evidence

Can support: causal involvement of a target gene when a wild-type gene restores a mutant phenotype.

Cannot support: detailed mechanism if construct design, expression level, or genetic background is unclear.

### biochemical/mechanistic evidence

Can support: mechanism-level evidence such as protein interaction, DNA binding, enzyme activity, transcriptional regulation, chromatin binding, or metabolic change.

Cannot support: sufficiency of the mechanism for the target trait without genetic phenotype or in vivo validation.

### field or breeding validation

Can support: applied value of a gene, allele, or molecular marker across field environments, breeding materials, or target populations.

Cannot support: complete molecular mechanism, and cannot ignore environmental effects or genetic background differences.

## 5. Literature Interpretation Notes

- Expression change is not functional validation.
- GO/KEGG enrichment is not mechanistic proof.
- qRT-PCR validation is not functional validation.
- Transient expression cannot fully replace stable genetic materials.
- Conclusions from one species or one treatment condition cannot be directly generalized to all plants.
- Overexpression phenotypes require attention to non-physiological expression levels.
- RNAi and VIGS require attention to off-target effects and silencing efficiency.
- CRISPR studies require attention to off-target effects, allele type, and genetic background.
- Field traits require separation of environmental effects, genotype effects, and genotype-by-environment interaction.
- Gene family members may show redundancy, divergence, or compensatory effects.
- Omics association, candidate gene discovery, and functional validation should be described as separate evidence layers.
- Selection signals do not by themselves prove adaptive function.
- Synteny or orthology does not guarantee conserved function across species.
- Population structure, demographic history, and linkage disequilibrium can confound genotype-trait associations.

## 6. Review Writing Guidance

- First classify studies by research direction, such as development, stress response, hormone signaling, yield and quality, nutrient uptake, or molecular breeding.
- Then compare studies by evidence level, from sequence annotation, expression correlation, network evidence, transient assays, genetic perturbation, complementation, mechanistic evidence, to field or breeding validation.
- Do not merely list gene names; state each gene's evidence type, experimental system, species background, and conclusion boundary.
- Summarize regulatory networks, shared mechanisms, species differences, and research gaps. Do not convert a single-paper result into a field-wide consensus.
- Mark unsupported points as `[source needed]`; mark available but unchecked support as `[needs verification]`.
- Use layered wording: source-reported content, reasonable inference, and model synthesis.
- Use different language for candidate genes and functionally validated genes. Strong causal wording should be reserved for evidence with genetic perturbation, complementation, or mechanistic support.
