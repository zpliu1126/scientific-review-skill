# Review Article Synthesis Reference

## 1. Scope

This file applies to narrative reviews, systematic reviews, meta-analyses, perspectives, mini-reviews, field overviews, and other review-type articles. Use it when interpreting review articles or when using review articles to support a user's own review writing.

Review articles are secondary sources. They synthesize, interpret, select, or statistically combine primary studies, but they should not be treated as equivalent to original research articles.

## 2. What Review Articles Can Support

Review articles can support:

- Research background and field context.
- Historical development of a field.
- Concept definitions and terminology.
- Classification of technologies, methods, pathways, models, or research directions.
- Summary of major research themes.
- Field-level consensus when the review clearly supports it.
- Areas of controversy or disagreement.
- Research gaps and future directions.
- Leads to key primary studies that should be traced and checked.

## 3. What Review Articles Cannot Support Alone

Do not rely only on a review article to support:

- Specific experimental results.
- Specific gene functions.
- Specific phenotype values or quantitative data.
- Treatment conditions, doses, time points, sample sizes, or statistical values.
- Mechanistic causality.
- Protein-protein interactions or protein-DNA interactions.
- Direct transcriptional regulation.
- GWAS/QTL candidate genes as causal genes.
- Clinical, therapeutic, agronomic, or breeding application value.

These claims should be traced to the relevant primary studies whenever possible.

## 4. Output Structure for Interpreting Review Articles

When reading a review article, structure the output as:

- Review type: narrative review, systematic review, meta-analysis, perspective, mini-review, or field overview.
- If the exact review type cannot be determined from the provided text, write `[needs verification]` or the user's requested equivalent marker.
- Review scope: topic, organisms, diseases, methods, time period, and evidence types covered.
- Core storyline: the main organizing logic of the article.
- Main points: key concepts, classifications, claims, or themes.
- Authors' synthesis: the authors' own integrative judgment or conceptual model.
- Content usable for the user's review: background, framing, terminology, classification, consensus, controversy, and gap statements.
- Content requiring primary-source tracing: experimental results, gene functions, phenotypes, mechanisms, candidate genes, clinical claims, or breeding claims.
- Limitations: selection bias, age of the review, missing search strategy, narrow scope, heterogeneity, or lack of primary-source verification.
- Writing guidance: how to cite or use the review in the user's own manuscript.

## 5. Evidence Use Rules

- Review synthesis is not primary evidence.
- Classification frameworks proposed by a review, such as grouping genes, mechanisms, pathways, or technologies into categories, are review synthesis rather than primary evidence.
- Experimental conclusions mentioned in a review should be traced to original research articles.
- Mechanism figures in review articles should be treated as the review authors' synthesis model unless each link is verified from primary sources.
- Narrative reviews may use selective citation and may not represent the full evidence base.
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses usually provide stronger secondary evidence than narrative reviews, but the search strategy, inclusion criteria, exclusion criteria, risk of bias, statistical model, heterogeneity, and publication bias must still be checked.
- Perspectives and mini-reviews are useful for framing and hypotheses, but they may be selective or opinion-driven.
- Outdated reviews should be flagged as requiring recent primary studies or newer reviews.
- Do not use a review article to upgrade weak primary evidence into strong causal evidence.
- Do not cite a review as the source for a specific experiment if the original primary article is needed.

## 6. Writing Guidance

- Cite review articles for background, field framing, conceptual definitions, method classification, historical context, broad consensus, controversy, or research gaps.
- Cite primary studies whenever making a specific experimental claim.
- Use review articles to identify important primary studies, then trace and verify those primary sources.
- Use cautious wording for summary claims from reviews, such as "has been summarized as", "is commonly discussed as", "the field has proposed", or "review authors suggest".
- Use `[review citation needed]` for statements that cite a review as background, framing, classification, or field synthesis.
- Mark claims lacking primary support as `[primary source needed]` or `[needs primary-source verification]`.
- Do not let a review article substitute for direct evidence when discussing gene function, phenotype, mechanism, clinical relevance, or breeding value.

Example:

```text
A review has summarized plant ABC transporters involved in abiotic stress responses into ion homeostasis, ABA signaling, ROS-related, and heavy-metal transport categories [review citation needed].
```
